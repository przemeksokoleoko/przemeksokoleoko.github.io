title: "The Same Hill, Different Maps: Perspectives in Project, Product, and Service Management"
---

Imagine three people standing at the base of the same hill, each holding a different map. The hill hasn't changed. But what each person sees, cares about, and worries about — that's entirely different. This is what it feels like to manage the same initiative through the lenses of project management, product management, and service management.

Let me use a concrete example to make this real.

---

## The Scenario: Launching an Internal Ticketing System

A company decides to roll out a new internal IT helpdesk ticketing system. It's a medium-sized initiative: there's a vendor tool being configured, integrations to build, people to train, and an existing manual process to replace.

Three people are involved:
- **Marta** is the Project Manager
- **Tomasz** is the Product Manager
- **Karolina** leads Service Management

Same hill. Three maps.

---

## Marta's Map: The Project Manager's Perspective

For Marta, the ticketing system rollout is a **bounded event with a clear end state**. She thinks in terms of:

- **Scope:** What exactly are we delivering? Which teams will use it? Which integrations are in this release?
- **Schedule:** When does it go live? What's the critical path? Are we at risk?
- **Resources:** Who does what? Is the developer overloaded? Do we have a vendor contact this week?
- **Risks and issues:** What might prevent delivery? What's already blocking us?

Marta's world is fundamentally about **predictability and delivery**. She runs weekly standups, tracks tasks on a Gantt chart or Kanban board, manages the stakeholder update cadence, and closes out the project when the system goes live and the team is trained.

When the go-live happens and the hypercare period ends, Marta marks the project as complete. She writes the lessons learned document, thanks the team, and moves on to the next initiative.

**What Marta doesn't focus on:** What happens after go-live. Whether users love it. Whether it's still meeting needs in six months.

That's not a criticism — it's the shape of her responsibility.

---

## Tomasz's Map: The Product Manager's Perspective

Tomasz sees the ticketing system as a **product with a lifecycle**. His questions are different:

- **Who are the users, and what do they actually need?** Not just "IT helpdesk" as a concept, but the 200 employees who will submit tickets and the 10 agents who will process them.
- **What outcomes are we trying to drive?** Faster resolution times? Fewer dropped requests? Less frustration?
- **What does success look like after three months? After a year?**
- **What's on the roadmap?** After the initial launch, what features or improvements should come next?

Tomasz thinks in **iterations and feedback loops**. He wants usage data. He'll set up a short survey after go-live. He'll talk to the support agents about their daily pain points. He's not satisfied when the system is deployed — he's tracking whether it's actually being adopted and whether it's delivering value.

For Tomasz, go-live is not the finish line. It's the beginning of the real work.

**What Tomasz doesn't focus on:** The day-to-day operational stability of the system. Whether SLAs are being met. Whether there's a process for escalations.

That's Karolina's territory.

---

## Karolina's Map: The Service Manager's Perspective

Karolina sees the ticketing system as a **service that must run reliably, day in and day out**. Her questions are different again:

- **What does "healthy" look like for this service?** What's the acceptable response time? What percentage of tickets should be resolved within 24 hours?
- **How do we handle incidents?** If the system goes down at 9am on a Monday, what's the escalation path?
- **How do we onboard new employees to using it?** Is there documentation? A quick-start guide?
- **How do we measure quality continuously?** Not just at launch, but every week.

Karolina's world is shaped by **ITIL-style thinking**: service levels, change management, configuration management, and continual improvement. She defines the SLA before go-live and sets up the review cadence after it. She's the one who builds the runbook for "what to do when the ticketing system itself breaks."

She's also the one who, six months later, is still paying attention — tracking ticket volumes, agent workload, escalation rates, and user satisfaction scores.

**What Karolina doesn't focus on:** Whether the product backlog is being prioritized correctly, or whether the project came in on time.

---

## Where the Maps Overlap (and Where They Don't)

These three perspectives aren't naturally in conflict — but they can easily become so if people don't recognize what each one is for.

| | Project Manager | Product Manager | Service Manager |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Time horizon** | Short (until done) | Medium-long (lifecycle) | Ongoing (always on) |
| **Success metric** | On time, on scope, on budget | Adoption, outcomes, value | Reliability, SLAs, satisfaction |
| **Primary question** | "Are we on track?" | "Are we building the right thing?" | "Is it running well?" |
| **Ends when?** | At project closure | Never (until decommission) | Never (until decommission) |

The most common friction I've seen:

- **Project vs. Product:** The project delivers, then "throws it over the wall." No one owns what happens next. Tomasz shows up asking for improvements and there's no clear backlog owner.
- **Project vs. Service:** The project cuts corners during handover. Karolina inherits a system with no documentation, no runbook, no defined SLAs.
- **Product vs. Service:** Tomasz wants to ship new features quickly. Karolina worries every change is a stability risk. They need a shared change management process.

---

## The Lesson: Perspective Shapes What You See

The ticketing system didn't change. But Marta, Tomasz, and Karolina are each holding a map optimized for a different kind of terrain.

None of them is wrong. All three perspectives are necessary. The failure mode isn't having different perspectives — it's assuming yours is the only one that matters, or not knowing the others exist at all.

The most effective organizations I've worked with aren't the ones that collapsed all three roles into one person (that just creates exhaustion). They're the ones that made the handoffs deliberate, the responsibilities explicit, and the conversations between these three people part of the normal operating rhythm.

Same hill. Different maps. But if you know what map the person next to you is using, you can actually navigate together.
